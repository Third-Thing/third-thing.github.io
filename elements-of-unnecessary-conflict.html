The Elements of Unnecessary Conflict

Unnecessary conflict arises from predictable communication missteps. They cluster into three parent categories (Assumptions, Lack of Clarity, and Lack of Considerateness) each with specific sub‑elements and practical remedies.

Assumptions

Assuming Shared Understanding

  • Cause – Treating your own meaning or goal as universally obvious.
  • Effect – The dialogue is now built on hidden gaps; participants are set up to talk past each other and later trade blame.
  • Alternative – Spell out your definition or experience and invite theirs.

Assuming Intentions (Mind‑Reading)

  • Cause – Inferring negative intent without verifying.
  • Effect – The interaction is now possibly shaped by false perception.
  • Alternative – Describe what you observe, note uncertainty, and ask for their view before reacting.

Lack of Clarity

Insufficient Justification

  • Cause – Stating a conclusion without the observations or logic that led to it.
  • Effect – With no reasoning trail, the conversation becomes a "trust‑me‑or‑doubt‑me" choice, inviting suspicion and challenge.
  • Alternative – Share the evidence chain from observation to conclusion (or question).

Not Stating What’s Important

  • Cause – Trying to motivate people without stating the need, desire, preference, standard, or priority that matters to you.
  • Effect – The conversation can turn into a guessing game about invisible standards, or a fight over negative judgments.
  • Alternative – Translate any judgments into a clear statement of what matters and why.

Lack of Considerateness

Implying or Asserting Deficiencies

  • Cause – Hinting or stating that the other person is stupid, incompetent, unethical, etc.
  • Effect – The interaction can shift from problem‑solving to self-image defense, making constructive exchange unlikely.
  • Alternative – Focus on what you're trying to accomplish. Offer alternatives. Rephrase if needed.

Unnecessarily Pointing Out Error

  • Cause – Opening with “You’re wrong” or a blunt correction.
  • Effect – Sets up a social dynamic around performative defense to save face.
  • Alternative – Ask questions or gently offer counter-evidence or an alternative perspective.
